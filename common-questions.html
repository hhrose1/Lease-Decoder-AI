Frequently Asked Tenant & Lease Questions

Important: Housing laws vary widely by state, city, and by the terms of each individual lease. This information is for general education only and does not constitute legal advice. For personalized assistance, contact local tenant legal aid using the Get Legal Help tool.

Before You Sign a Lease

What should I look for before signing a lease?
Confirm what you are responsible for and what your landlord must provide, including: rent amount and schedule, utilities, late-fee policy, security deposit rules, guest/pet/parking rules, maintenance responsibilities, mold/pest responsibilities, and early termination options. Request all agreements in writing before signing.

Do I need a walkthrough? What should I check?
Yes — inspect everything before move-in and document concerns with photos. Look for: leaks, soft drywall, musty smells (mold), pests, broken locks or windows, and malfunctioning appliances. Email your landlord photos the same day for a paper trail.

Should I ask existing tenants about the property?
Yes. Ask about maintenance responsiveness, safety at night, pests, cleanliness, noise levels, and whether management is easy to contact. If multiple tenants report the same issue — take it seriously.

Do I need renters insurance?
Usually required — always recommended. Protects your belongings and liability. Typical cost: $10–20/month.

Can a landlord check my credit? What if it’s low?
Yes. If credit is low, you can offer alternatives like a co-signer, proof of income, or savings. Each landlord sets their own acceptance criteria.

Once You Move In

How should I request repairs?
Always submit requests in writing (email is best) with photos and a clear timeline. Keep copies of every message and response.

What makes a rental “unsafe” or “uninhabitable”?
Anything that impacts health or safety can qualify — major leaks, lack of heat or water, severe mold, pests, faulty wiring, or broken doors/windows that prevent secure entry.

Rent, Fees & Deposits

What is rent escrow?
A legal process where tenants pay rent to the court instead of the landlord when major repair issues are ignored. Do not withhold rent without legal guidance — laws differ by state.

Roommates & Neighbors

What if my roommate breaks the lease?
You may still be financially responsible. Consider a written roommate agreement and communicate lease terms clearly before signing.

Lease Changes, Renewals & Moving Out

Can I break my lease early?
Usually — but often with fees unless state protections apply (safety concerns, DV, military orders, etc.). Ask whether the landlord allows replacement tenants and what notice is required.

How do leases typically renew?
Options include:

  • Automatic renewal — continues unless you give notice
  • Fixed-term renewal — usually new 12-month contract
  • Month-to-month — more flexible, but rent may change
Always ask about notice deadlines and expected rent increases.

Evictions & Legal Rights

What is eviction?
A court-ordered process for removing a tenant. You must receive written notice and a court date. Landlords cannot lock you out or remove belongings without a court order.

Where can I get help?
Tenant legal organizations, university legal clinics, and housing advocacy groups. Use the Get Legal Help tool for services in your area.

General Best Practices

  • Keep all communication in writing
  • Photograph and document all issues
  • Ask for clarification before signing anything
  • Seek help early — problems worsen when delayed
